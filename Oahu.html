Overview

Five days covering O'ahu's three distinct personalities. You land Monday afternoon and ease into Waikiki. Day 2 tackles the classic Honolulu circuit — Diamond Head at sunrise, the Bishop Museum, and Manoa Valley. Day 3 crosses to the windward coast for Makapu'u, Hanauma Bay snorkeling, and a kayak to the Mokulua Islands. Day 4 pushes inland through Ko'olau country — Koko Head stairs, Kualoa Ranch, the Byodo-In Temple, and the Pali Lookout. Day 5 ends the O'ahu chapter on the legendary North Shore: pillbox hike, Waimea Valley waterfall, shave ice in Haleiwa, and Friday night fireworks back in Waikiki.

Arrive HNL & Pick Up Car
1:42 pm · UA 219 from ORD · CONRAC rental facility
Land, collect bags, pick up the rental car and drive to the hotel. About 20 minutes to Waikiki without traffic — allow 35 in the afternoon.
+

Rental car counters are in the consolidated facility (CONRAC) — follow signs after baggage claim. The shuttle to CONRAC runs every few minutes. The H-1 freeway gets you to Waikiki in 20 minutes under normal conditions; afternoon traffic can push this to 35–40 minutes. Hotel parking in Waikiki typically runs $35–55/night valet or $25–40 self-park. Cheaper garages can be found one block inland on Ala Wai Boulevard.

TipPre-book your rental and use a card with CDW (collision damage waiver) coverage — saves significantly at the counter. Don't nap when you arrive: push through to local bedtime to beat jet lag.
Waikiki Beach Walk
Kalakaua Ave · Walk from hotel · Free
The iconic strip — Duke Kahanamoku statue, gentle waves, Diamond Head on the horizon. The perfect first-evening orientation.
+

After dropping bags, walk Kalakaua Avenue along the beach. The Duke Kahanamoku statue (the father of modern surfing) sits at the center of the strip and is the traditional first photo stop. Diamond Head looms at the east end — the same crater you'll hike tomorrow morning at 6:30am. The beach is walkable from any Waikiki hotel and the late-afternoon light is ideal. Waikiki's beach is about 2 miles long with consistently warm, gentle surf suitable for swimming.

TipThe stretch in front of the Moana Surfrider and Royal Hawaiian hotels has the best sand and the classic O'ahu beach view. Grab a drink at one of the open-air beach bars and watch the sunset over Diamond Head.
Leonard's Bakery
933 Kapahulu Ave · Open daily 5:30am–9pm · ~$2–4 each
The original Honolulu malasada — Portuguese fried dough dusted in sugar, a Hawaii institution since 1952. The required first-night dessert.
+

Leonard's has been the Honolulu malasada standard since 1952. The plain sugar version is the classic — crispy outside, pillowy inside. They also do malasada puffs filled with custard, guava, or haupia (coconut). It's a 10-minute drive from central Waikiki on Kapahulu Avenue. There's usually a short queue that moves fast. Get them fresh and hot straight from the fryer.

TipThe plain sugar malasadas are best eaten immediately. If taking some back to the hotel, the filled puffs hold better. They can sell out late in the evening — don't leave it until after 8pm.

Tuesday, May 19
Diamond Head, Bishop Museum & Manoa
Waikiki / Kailua — Night 2
Stops
Diamond Head State Monument
6:30 am · $5/person + $10 parking · Book at hawaiistateparks.org
The classic O'ahu summit — 1.6 miles round trip up a volcanic tuff cone with sweeping views over Waikiki at sunrise. Book well in advance.
+

Le'ahi (Diamond Head) is the most recognizable natural landmark in Hawaii. The 1.6-mile round trip gains 560 feet through a mix of paved path, steep staircases, a spiral staircase inside an old fire control station, and a short lighted tunnel through the crater wall. The summit lookout delivers a 360-degree view: Waikiki below, the Ko'olau Mountains inland, and open Pacific in every direction. A 6:30am entry puts you at the top near sunrise before the heat and crowds arrive — by 9am both the parking lot and trail are packed.

TipReservations are mandatory for non-residents: hawaiistateparks.org. The parking lot is small and time-slotted — book the first available slot. No water on trail; bring at least 1 liter. Closed-toe shoes recommended for the stairs.
Bishop Museum
9:30 am · 1525 Bernice St · $38.95/adult · Open 9am–5pm
The Smithsonian of Hawaii — 23 million artifacts spanning Hawaiian history, Polynesian culture, and Pacific natural science. Allow 2–3 hours.
+

Founded in 1889 and holding the world's largest collection of Hawaiian cultural artifacts, the Bishop Museum is an essential stop for understanding what you're seeing across the islands. The centerpiece Hawaiian Hall has three stories of balconies lined with royal feather capes, koa wood canoes, navigation tools, and ancient ceremonial objects — with a sperm whale skeleton suspended from the ceiling. The Science Adventure Center has a live volcano simulation worth seeing. Allow 2 to 2.5 hours for a thorough visit.

TipNo reservation required, but school groups arrive on weekday mornings — 9:30am entry gets ahead of them. The $38.95 admission is genuinely worthwhile. The gift shop carries excellent books on Hawaiian history and hand-crafted local items.
Lyon Arboretum
1:00 pm · 3860 Manoa Rd · Free · Mon–Fri 9am–3pm only
A 200-acre University of Hawaii tropical botanical garden tucked into Manoa Valley — cool, lush, and completely different from Waikiki just 15 minutes away.
+

The Harold L. Lyon Arboretum is a University of Hawaii research garden covering 194 acres of native and tropical plants — heliconias, orchids, gingers, native Hawaiian ethnobotanical collections, and a dense forest of palms and tree ferns. The valley receives significantly more rainfall than Waikiki and feels like a different climate entirely. Trails wind through several themed garden areas at a leisurely pace. Free to visit; RSVP online recommended (lyonarboretum.manoa.hawaii.edu).

TipMon–Fri only; closed weekends and state holidays. Bring bug spray — the lush environment means mosquitoes. The arboretum connects to the Manoa Falls trailhead just up the road.
Manoa Falls Trail
3:30 pm · $5 parking · 1.6 mi round trip · Closed Mondays
An easy 1.6-mile round trip through dense rainforest to a 150-foot waterfall — just 15 minutes from Waikiki but feels like a different island entirely.
+

The Manoa Falls Trail follows Manoa Stream through a lush jungle of banyan trees, bamboo, and enormous tree ferns to a 150-foot waterfall plunging into a pool at the valley head. The trail is well-maintained and easy to follow; the main challenge is mud — the valley is one of the wettest spots on O'ahu and the trail surface stays damp year-round. The forest canopy is dense and the setting genuinely dramatic, used as a filming location for Jurassic Park and The Hunger Games: Catching Fire.

TipWaterproof closed-toe shoes are essential — sandals are a bad idea. Swimming at the falls is not allowed (leptospirosis risk from stream water). The trail is closed Mondays. $5 parking at the trailhead lot; free street parking a few blocks down.

Southeast Coast — Hanauma Bay & Kailua
Waikiki / Kailua — Night 3
Stops
Makapu'u Lighthouse Trail
7:00 am · Free · 2 mi paved round trip · Open 7am–7:45pm
A paved 2-mile round trip to O'ahu's easternmost point — sweeping views of the windward coast, Rabbit Island offshore, and the Ko'olau Range. No reservation needed.
+

The Makapu'u Lighthouse Trail follows a paved service road up the sea cliffs at O'ahu's eastern tip, offering panoramic views at every turn of the route. The windward coastline of O'ahu unrolls to the north from the top: Kailua Bay, the Mokulua Islands, and the unbroken Ko'olau ridgeline. The historic red-and-white lighthouse sits below the trail on the point. A side trail descends to tidepools and the base of the cliff. The trail is fully accessible and stroller-friendly with no technical sections.

TipArrive by 7am — the parking lot fills by 8am on weekdays and earlier on weekends. No shade on the trail: bring water and sunscreen. Morning light is ideal on the east-facing cliffs.
Waiāhole Beach Park
~9:30 am · Kamehameha Hwy · Free · Quick stop
A hidden windward coast gem — black volcanic sand tucked against lush mountains, almost no crowds. Worth a 20-minute stop between Makapu'u and Hanauma Bay.
+

Waiāhole Beach Park sits on the windward coast just north of the junction back toward Hanauma Bay. The dark volcanic sand against turquoise water and the lush mountain wall behind it is a stark visual contrast to Waikiki's white sand. The beach is quiet, local, and has no facilities — bring water and snacks. It's a 20-minute stop maximum but it's the kind of O'ahu moment most visitors never see.

TipFree roadside parking along Kamehameha Hwy. No restrooms. The water can have a strong current here — better for looking than swimming. The scenery is the point.
Hanauma Bay Nature Preserve
11:00 am · $25/person · Wed–Sun only · Book 2 days ahead at 7am exactly
O'ahu's premier snorkeling site — a protected marine sanctuary inside a volcanic crater with calm water, abundant reef fish, and green sea turtles.
+

Hanauma Bay is a 300,000-year-old collapsed volcanic crater open to the ocean, now one of Hawaii's most protected marine sanctuaries. The sheltered shape creates exceptionally calm, clear water with a fringing reef starting just 15–20 feet from shore. Marine life is abundant: humuhumunukunukuapua'a (the state fish), parrotfish, butterflyfish, moray eels, and Hawaiian green sea turtles are all regular sightings. All visitors must watch a mandatory 9-minute educational video before entering the water. Snorkel gear is available on site (~$25 rental). The bay closes Mondays and Tuesdays.

TipReservations open at exactly 7:00am, two days in advance at hanaumabaystatepark.org — slots sell out within minutes. Set a phone alarm for May 18 at 7:00am HST to book for May 20. Non-mineral sunscreen (oxybenzone, octinoxate) is banned — wear a rash guard or use zinc oxide. Arrive at your booked time slot.
Kailua Beach + Mokulua Islands Kayak
3:00 pm · Beach free · Kayak $30–50/hr · Twogood Kayaks
O'ahu's most beautiful beach — powdery white sand and turquoise water — with an optional kayak to the twin Moke Islands offshore. One of the best afternoons on the island.
+

Kailua Beach is consistently ranked among the best beaches in the United States — 2.5 miles of fine white sand, shallow turquoise water warmed by the trade winds, and the dramatic Ko'olau Mountains as a backdrop. About a mile offshore, the twin Mokulua Islands (the "Mokes") are a seabird sanctuary accessible by kayak. Kayaking out takes 20–30 minutes each way; you can land on Moku Nui (the larger island) and explore the beach and nesting bird colony. Twogood Kayaks is located at 134B Hamakua Drive, Kailua — a short walk from the park.

TipTrade winds pick up in the afternoon, making the return paddle harder. Aim to reach the islands before 1pm and start heading back by 2pm. Plan to arrive at Kailua Beach by 3pm for a relaxed late-afternoon swim even if you skip the kayak. Bring reef-safe sunscreen and a dry bag for your phone.

Thursday, May 21
Koko Head, Kualoa Ranch & the Pali
Waikiki / Kailua — Night 4
Stops
Koko Head Crater Trail
6:00 am · Free · 1,048 steps straight up · No reservation
O'ahu's most brutal short hike — 1,048 wooden railway ties bolted directly up the steep crater wall. No switchbacks, no shade, no mercy. Extraordinary at the top.
+

Koko Head is the physically demanding hike of the O'ahu trip — 1,048 wooden railway ties (remnants of a WWII military tramway) bolted straight up the outer wall of a volcanic crater. The climb is direct: no switchbacks, no flat sections, 930 feet of elevation gain over 0.45 miles. There is one exposed section mid-climb where the railway bridge crosses a gap — hands and feet required, but it's stable. At the top: 360-degree views spanning Hanauma Bay, the entire southeast coast, Maunalua Bay, and the Ko'olau range. Round trip takes 45–60 minutes depending on pace.

TipStart at 6am — the stairs face southeast and the sun hits the exposed face early. By 8am in May it's hot and crowded. Bring plenty of water and real shoes; sandals will destroy your feet. Free parking at Koko Head District Park.
Kualoa Ranch
10:00 am · 49-560 Kamehameha Hwy · UTV from $167 · Book at kualoa.com
4,000 acres of sacred Hawaiian valley — film site for Jurassic Park, LOST, and Kong: Skull Island. The UTV tour drives you deep into the jungle interior.
+

Kualoa Ranch is a working cattle ranch set in Ka'a'awa Valley on O'ahu's windward coast — 4,000 acres of dramatic landscape between sheer Ko'olau ridgelines and the ocean. The property has been a film location for over 70 productions including Jurassic Park (1993), Jurassic World, Godzilla, LOST, and Kong: Skull Island. The UTV (side-by-side utility vehicle) experience puts you behind the wheel on dirt trails through the jungle valleys with guides pointing out filming locations, native plants, and Hawaiian history. Tours run approximately 2–2.5 hours.

TipBook at kualoa.com well in advance — popular experiences sell out weeks ahead. The UTV tour starts at $167/person; a valid driver's license is required to drive. Closed-toe shoes required. The valley can be muddy — don't wear anything you care about.
Byodo-In Temple
2:30 pm · 47-200 Kahekili Hwy · $7/adult · Open 8:30am–4:30pm
A full-scale replica of a 900-year-old Japanese temple set against a sheer 2,000-foot Ko'olau mountain wall. One of O'ahu's most striking and serene moments.
+

Built in 1968 to mark the 100th anniversary of Japanese immigration to Hawaii, the Byodo-In is a full-scale replica of the original Hōō-dō temple in Uji, Japan — same proportions, lacquered red wood, two-ton brass bell, and a massive gold-and-lacquer Buddha inside. What makes it exceptional is the setting: a sheer 2,000-foot Ko'olau mountain wall covered in green tropical vegetation, often wreathed in mist, rising directly behind the temple. Black swans and peacocks wander the koi-filled grounds. Remove shoes before entering the temple.

TipThe temple is inside Valley of the Temples Memorial Park — $7/adult entry, last entrance 4:15pm. The late-afternoon cloud shadows moving across the Ko'olau ridge make for ideal photos. Speak quietly; this is an active place of prayer.
Nu'uanu Pali Lookout
4:30 pm · $7 parking · Open daily 9am–4pm · Closes at 4pm sharp
O'ahu's most dramatic viewpoint — a 1,200-foot cliff where Kamehameha unified the Hawaiian Islands in 1795. Trade winds are strong enough to push you backward.
+

The Nu'uanu Pali Lookout sits at 1,200 feet on the Ko'olau Ridge where the cliffs drop nearly 1,000 feet to the windward plain below. It was here in 1795 that King Kamehameha I's forces drove O'ahu warriors off the cliff's edge, unifying the Hawaiian Islands under a single ruler. The view takes in the entire windward coast from Kailua Bay south to the Kahalu'u peninsula north — one of the grandest panoramas on O'ahu. The trade winds funnel through the Ko'olau gap here with remarkable force.

TipThe lot closes at 4pm — plan carefully, especially after the Byodo-In. Hold onto anything loose in the wind. The old Pali Highway (accessible by foot) offers an alternative viewpoint if the main lot is full.

Friday, May 22
North Shore — Pillbox, Waimea & Haleiwa
Waikiki / Kailua — Night 5
Stops
Ehukai Pillbox Trail
9:00 am · Free · Park near Sunset Elementary · ~1 hr round trip
A short, steep scramble to WWII-era concrete lookout bunkers above Sunset Beach — panoramic views of the entire North Shore coastline in both directions.
+

The Ehukai Pillbox hike climbs to a pair of WWII military observation bunkers perched on a ridgeline above Sunset Beach — the world-famous big-wave surf break that makes headlines every winter. The trail is short (about 0.5 miles each way) but involves rope-assisted scrambling on loose red dirt. The payoff is a 180-degree view of the entire North Shore: Sunset Beach directly below, Ehukai Beach, the distant mountains, and the offshore reef breaks. In late May the North Shore is calm with smaller summer swells — very different from the 20–40 foot winter faces.

TipPark near Sunset Beach Elementary School (59-360 Kamehameha Hwy). Wear shoes with grip — the red dirt is treacherous when wet. Go in the morning before the trail gets crowded and the ridge sun gets hot. The 9am Sunrise Shack on the way out is an ideal first stop.
Waimea Valley
11:00 am · 59-864 Kamehameha Hwy · $25/adult · Open daily · Check waimeavalley.net
A 1.5-mile walk through a botanical garden to a swimmable 45-foot waterfall — one of the most beautiful easy walks on O'ahu.
+

Waimea Valley is a 1,875-acre nature park and cultural center holding O'ahu's most intact ahupua'a (traditional Hawaiian land division). The main trail follows the valley floor through botanical garden collections — ethnobotanical plants, native Hawaiian species, and peacocks roaming freely — for about 0.75 miles each way to Waihi Falls, a 45-foot waterfall with a swimmable pool at the base. Life jackets are provided and required for the swim. The lifeguard makes the daily swim call at 9am. Allow 2 to 2.5 hours for the walk, a swim, and cultural exhibits.

TipEntry is $25/adult. Parking is included. Arrive when it opens — bus tours arrive mid-morning and the valley fills quickly. The waterfall swim is the highlight; check weather before going as it can be closed after heavy rain.
Kawela Bay + Sunset Beach
2:30 pm · Free · Kamehameha Hwy
Kawela Bay is a calm, tree-lined bay hidden past Turtle Bay Resort — ideal for a quiet swim. Sunset Beach is the legendary big-wave break, calmer and swimmable in May.
+

Kawela Bay, just past Turtle Bay Resort, is one of the North Shore's most peaceful spots — a small, sheltered bay lined with ironwood trees, almost entirely skipped by tourists. It's ideal for a calm swim or just sitting in the shade. Sunset Beach itself is the world-renowned big-wave site that hosts the Triple Crown of Surfing each winter; in May the same beach is calm and swimmable, and the setting — wide beach, clear water, the Ko'olau mountains behind — is still extraordinary.

TipKawela Bay is accessed just past the Turtle Bay Resort entrance off Kamehameha Hwy — look for the roadside parking. No facilities at Kawela Bay; Sunset Beach has restrooms and showers. Both are free.
Haleiwa Town + Matsumoto Shave Ice
4:30 pm · 66-111 Kamehameha Hwy · Daily 9am–6pm
The soul of the North Shore — surf shops, galleries, plate lunch spots, and the legendary Matsumoto Shave Ice that has been here since 1951.
+

Haleiwa is the de facto capital of the North Shore — a small historic town that has retained a distinctly local character despite heavy tourism. The main strip has surf shops selling boards and wax alongside galleries, clothing boutiques, and places to just sit and watch North Shore life. Matsumoto Shave Ice (founded 1951) is the benchmark O'ahu shave ice: machine-shaved ultra-fine ice topped with house-made syrups. Order a "special" with azuki beans in the bottom and a scoop of ice cream in the center. Giovanni's Shrimp Truck, the original North Shore shrimp truck, is parked nearby.

TipMatsumoto is at 66-111 Kamehameha Hwy — easy to find by the line. Giovanni's Shrimp Truck (66-472 Kamehameha Hwy) for scampi shrimp over rice is the classic pairing. Leave Haleiwa by 5:30pm to return for the 7:45pm fireworks in Waikiki (~55 min drive).
Friday Night Fireworks, Waikiki
7:45 pm · Hilton Hawaiian Village · Every Friday · Free
Honolulu's Friday tradition — a fireworks show launched over the ocean from the Hilton Hawaiian Village beach, visible across the entire Waikiki shoreline. May 22 is a Friday.
+

Every Friday evening at 7:45pm, the Hilton Hawaiian Village launches fireworks over the ocean — a tradition that has become the signature weekly event on O'ahu. The show runs about 10–15 minutes and is visible from the Hilton's own beach (best view), from anywhere along the Waikiki shoreline, and from hotel balconies facing west. May 22 falls on a Friday — a perfect alignment for the last full night of the O'ahu leg. The entire Waikiki strip takes on a celebratory atmosphere; bars set up outdoor seating and the beach fills with locals and visitors.

TipClaim a spot on Waikiki Beach by 7:15pm. Any patch of sand between the Hilton and the Outrigger Waikiki delivers a great view. Leave Haleiwa by 5:30pm to allow for the return drive and parking before showtime.

Practical Notes

Rental Car
Pick up at HNL on arrival May 18. Essential from Day 2 onward — O'ahu's bus system is too slow for the distances involved. Pre-book for the best rates; use a card with CDW coverage to avoid paying the counter add-on. Hotel parking in Waikiki is $25–55/night — cheaper garages are one block inland on Ala Wai Blvd.
Hanauma Bay
Reservations open at exactly 7:00am, two days in advance, at hanaumabaystatepark.org. Set a phone alarm for May 18 at 7am HST. Slots sell out in 2–3 minutes — don't be late. Non-mineral sunscreen (oxybenzone, octinoxate) is banned — wear a rash guard or use zinc-oxide sunscreen. Closed Mon & Tue.
Diamond Head
Book at hawaiistateparks.org — $5/person + $10 parking. The parking lot is small, time-slotted, and strictly enforced. A 6:30am entry gets you near sunrise with minimal crowds. No water on trail; bring at least 1 liter. Closed-toe shoes recommended for the staircase sections.
Kualoa Ranch
Book the UTV Experience at kualoa.com well ahead of travel — sells out weeks in advance. Valid driver's license required to operate the UTV. Wear closed-toe shoes. The valley can be muddy after rain — don't wear anything you'd mind ruining. Tours run approximately 2–2.5 hours.
Reef-Safe Sunscreen
Hawaii law bans sunscreens containing oxybenzone and octinoxate. This is strictly enforced at Hanauma Bay where non-mineral sunscreen is prohibited entirely — wear a UPF rash guard instead. Use mineral-based (zinc oxide or titanium dioxide) sunscreen across all five days. Standard mainland sunscreens may result in fines at managed sites.
North Shore Timing
The drive from Waikiki to Sunset Beach takes 45–55 minutes via H-2. The Ehukai Pillbox and Waimea Valley are both best in the morning before heat and bus tours. Leave Haleiwa by 5:30pm to return for the 7:45pm Friday fireworks in Waikiki — allow 50–55 minutes for the return drive.
Pali Lookout
Closes at 4pm sharp — the lot is gated and there is no flexibility. Plan Day 4's afternoon with this in mind: leave Kualoa Ranch by 2pm, arrive Byodo-In by 2:30pm, Pali by 4:15pm latest. The old Pali Highway (accessible on foot) is a backup if the lot is full.
Muddy Trails
Both Manoa Falls (Day 2) and Ehukai Pillbox (Day 5) can be extremely muddy — the red volcanic dirt becomes slippery clay when wet. Waterproof closed-toe shoes are essential for both. Sandals will not work. Check weather the night before; heavy overnight rain makes the pillbox trail significantly harder.

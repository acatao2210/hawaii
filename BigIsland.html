Overview

This itinerary covers both the west (Kona) and east (Hilo) sides of the Big Island over five days. You arrive on the 23rd for a relaxed first evening in Kona. Day 7 takes you up the Kohala Coast for snorkeling and the coffee farm. Day 8 crosses to the east side via the north — Waipio Valley and Akaka Falls — before checking into Hilo. Day 9 is the big east-side day: Volcanoes in the morning and Mauna Kea in the evening. Day 10 loops around the southern tip — Green Sand Beach and Punalu'u — before driving up the west coast to Kona for the final night.

Saturday, May 23
Arrive & Settle, Kona
Kona — Night 1
Stops
Kailua-Kona Town
Alii Drive · Walk from most hotels
The main strip of Kona — waterfront restaurants, coffee shops, local boutiques and the Hulihee Palace. A low-key intro to the island.
+

Kailua-Kona is the social heart of the Big Island's west side, centered on Alii Drive — a breezy oceanfront strip lined with open-air restaurants, coffee bars pouring 100% Kona, surf shops, and small galleries. It's an easy walking area once you park. The historic Hulihee Palace (a 19th-century royal vacation home) sits right on the waterfront and is worth a look. Afternoon arrival gives you time to get your bearings, grab lunch, and explore at a gentle pace before the trip kicks into gear tomorrow.

TipParking on Alii Drive fills fast — use the free lot on Kuakini Hwy and walk down. The stretch between Hulihe'e Palace and the Coconut Grove Marketplace has the best concentration of restaurants and is very walkable.
Kahaluu Beach Park
2 mi south of Kona · Open 7am–8pm · Free parking
One of the best and most accessible snorkel spots on the island — calm, shallow reef teeming with turtles and tropical fish. A short drive from town.
+

Kahaluu Beach Park is consistently rated one of the top snorkel sites in Hawaii for accessibility — the reef starts just a few feet from shore, the water is calm, and the marine life is abundant. Hawaiian green sea turtles are regular visitors, often grazing on the algae-covered rocks within arm's reach (keep a respectful distance). Reef fish in every color are everywhere. The beach itself is small with a black-lava shoreline, but there's sand for lounging, restrooms, and a surf shack that rents snorkel gear if you don't have your own.

TipArrive before 10am for the calmest water and fewest people. Entry to the water is over a rocky shelf — water shoes are helpful. Snorkel gear rental is available at the beach for around $15.

West Side — Snorkel & Coffee Farm
Kona — Night 2
Stops
Kohala Divers — Snorkel / Dive
Kawaihae Harbor  ·  Open 8am–5pm  ·  Rated 4.9 stars
Small-boat morning snorkel trip along the pristine Kohala Coast reef. Spinner dolphins, sea turtles, and moray eels common in May.
+

One of the best-reviewed dive and snorkel operators on the Big Island, Kohala Divers runs small-boat trips to pristine reef sites along the Kohala Coast — an area known for exceptional water clarity, healthy coral, and abundant marine life. Morning snorkel trips typically visit two sites and last 2–3 hours. Common sightings include spinner dolphins, green sea turtles, octopus, moray eels, and a wide variety of reef fish. Spinner dolphins are frequently encountered in May, often riding the bow wave. Non-certified divers and non-swimmers can snorkel at the same sites while certified divers go deeper.

TipBook well in advance — morning slots fill up weeks ahead, especially in late May. Morning boat slots start at 8am. Confirm exact departure time when booking.
Heavenly Hawaiian Kona Coffee Farm
Holualoa hills · Open until 4:30pm
Guided tour of a working 100% Kona coffee farm with five tastings and sweeping Pacific views. Rated 4.9 stars.
+

A working 100% Kona coffee farm set on the slopes above the Kona coast with sweeping Pacific views. Guided one-hour tours walk you through the entire process — from cherry to roast — with five different coffee tastings included. The farm grows its own arabica on volcanic soil in the famous Kona coffee belt, one of the few places in the US where coffee is commercially grown. The gift shop carries single-origin bags you won't find elsewhere. Rated 4.9 stars across nearly 3,000 reviews.

TipLast tour of the day typically runs at 3:30pm. Book in advance online; walk-ins are accepted but slots fill quickly in peak season.

Monday, May 25
North Loop — Akaka Falls & Waipio → Hilo
Hilo — Night 3
Stops
Waipio Valley Lookout
Hamakua Coast  ·  Free  ·  No hiking required
A sheer 2,000-foot green wall dropping to a black sand beach. Hawaii's most sacred valley and birthplace of Kamehameha I's power.
+

Waipio ("curving water") is the largest and most historically significant of the Big Island's amphitheater valleys, stretching a mile wide and six miles deep. It was a sacred gathering place for Hawaiian royalty and the birthplace of Kamehameha I's political power. The lookout at the valley's rim provides one of the most dramatic panoramas in all of Hawaii: a sheer 2,000-foot green wall dropping to a black sand beach, a winding river, and taro fields far below. The road into the valley grades at 25% in places — rental cars are not suited for it.

TipDo not attempt to drive into the valley with a standard rental car — the grade is genuinely dangerous and tow trucks charge significantly for extraction.
'Akaka Falls State Park
Honomu, near Hilo  ·  Open 8:30am–5pm  ·  $5/vehicle
A 442-foot free-fall waterfall in a jungle of bamboo, heliconia, and tree ferns. A paved 30-minute loop — entirely different from west-side terrain.
+

'Akaka Falls is one of the most spectacular waterfalls in Hawaii — a single, uninterrupted 442-foot free-fall plunging into a lush jungle gorge. The park's paved 0.4-mile loop trail winds through a dense canopy of bamboo, heliconia, ginger, and enormous tree ferns, passing a smaller 100-foot fall (Kahuna Falls) before reaching the main overlook. The trail takes 30–45 minutes at a leisurely pace and is accessible for most fitness levels.

TipThe loop trail is paved but has uneven sections and some steps. The overlook platform can be slippery when wet. Bring bug spray — the jungle environment means mosquitoes.

Tuesday, May 26
Volcanoes & Mauna Kea
Hilo — Night 4
Stops
Hawai'i Volcanoes National Park
Kilauea Caldera  ·  Open 24 hrs  ·  $30/vehicle (7-day pass)
Morning visit — cooler air, fewer crowds, steam vents at their most dramatic. Kilauea Iki trail, lava tube, Chain of Craters Road.
+

Home to one of the most active volcanoes on Earth, this 333,000-acre park is unlike any other in the national park system. A morning start from Hilo means you arrive before the tour buses, the light is better in the crater, and the cooler temperatures make the Kilauea Iki hike much more comfortable. Recommended sequence: Kilauea Iki trail (4 miles, moderate — descends across a hardened lava lake with steam vents), Thurston Lava Tube (500-year-old lava tunnel, easy 20-minute walk), then Chain of Craters Road down to the coast for dramatic lava field views. Aim to be done by noon.

TipThe $30 entry fee covers 7 days with in-and-out privileges. Sulfur dioxide gas levels vary — check the NPS air quality report before the visit and avoid the caldera rim if vog is high.
Mauna Kea Summit & Stargazing
13,796 ft summit  ·  Visitor Center open 9am–9:30pm
The tallest mountain on Earth from base to peak. Sunset above the cloud layer and the Milky Way to the naked eye.
+

Mauna Kea is the tallest mountain on Earth when measured from its oceanic base, and its summit sits above 40% of Earth's atmosphere — making it home to the world's most powerful concentration of astronomical observatories. Arrive at the Visitor Center well before sunset to acclimatize, then drive to the summit for the sunset (breathtaking above the cloud layer) and stay for stargazing. The Milky Way is visible to the naked eye, and the Visitor Center offers free public stargazing programs with telescopes most clear evenings starting around 6pm.

TipTemperature at the summit averages 33°F regardless of valley conditions. Bring full warm layers. Allow a minimum of 30 minutes at the Visitor Center (9,200 ft) before proceeding — altitude sickness is a real risk. Most rental car agreements prohibit the summit road; confirm before assuming you can drive it, or book a guided stargazing tour.

South Loop — Green Sand & Black Sand
Kona — Night 5
Stops
Punalu'u Black Sand Beach
Ka'ū District  ·  Open 24 hrs  ·  Free parking
Jet-black volcanic sand, Hawaiian green sea turtles resting on shore, and a wild Pacific backdrop.
+

One of the most visually dramatic beaches in Hawaii, Punalu'u is a broad expanse of jet-black volcanic sand formed by lava flowing into the sea and shattering on contact with cold water. Hawaiian green sea turtles (honu) regularly haul themselves onto the beach to rest — it's common to find a dozen or more at any given time. The juxtaposition of black sand, turquoise water, and lush green palms makes it one of the most photographed spots on the island.

TipFederal law requires you to stay at least 10 feet from resting turtles. Keep noise low and do not approach from the water side.
Papakōlea Green Sand Beach
South Point (Ka Lae)  ·  2.5 mi hike each way
One of only a handful of green sand beaches on Earth — its color comes from olivine crystals eroded from the surrounding cinder cone.
+

One of only a handful of green sand beaches on Earth, Papakōlea gets its distinctive color from olivine crystals — a semi-precious mineral — eroded from the surrounding cinder cone. The beach sits inside a collapsed volcanic crater accessible only on foot or by rough-road vehicle. The hike is 2.5 miles each way across open, shadeless scrubland with consistent trade winds and May sun — bring far more water than you think you need. The payoff is remarkable: emerald-green sand, turquoise water, and sea turtles in the shallows.

TipLocal residents operate informal truck shuttles from the trailhead parking area for roughly $20–25 cash per person each way. Widely used and saves significant time. Bring cash.

Practical Notes

Rental Car
A standard rental covers everything except Green Sand Beach (4WD to drive in — hike or take a local truck) and Mauna Kea summit (most agreements prohibit the summit road). Confirm Mauna Kea access with your rental company or book a guided stargazing tour.
Volcanoes National Park
Entry is $30/vehicle, valid for 7 days. Allow 4–5 hours minimum. Do Kilauea Iki trail (4 mi, moderate), walk the Thurston Lava Tube, and drive Chain of Craters Road. Stay until dark for the lava glow — it intensifies significantly after sunset.
Mauna Kea
Stop at the Visitor Center (9,200 ft) for at least 30 minutes before proceeding to the 13,796 ft summit. Summit temperature averages 33°F — bring full warm layers, not just a fleece. Anyone with heart or respiratory conditions should consult a doctor before ascending.
Green Sand Beach
The hike is 2.5 miles each way across exposed, shadeless terrain — bring at least 2 liters of water per person. Local residents operate informal truck shuttles from the parking area for approximately $20–25 cash per person each way.
Kohala Divers
Book well in advance — morning slots fill up weeks ahead. They offer snorkel and dive trips; non-divers and non-certified swimmers can snorkel at the same reef sites. Dolphins are frequently sighted in May.
Packing Notes
Reef-safe sunscreen (required in Hawaii), sturdy closed-toe shoes for Volcanoes and lava fields, warm layers for Mauna Kea, cash for Green Sand Beach truck ($20–25), and a headlamp for the lava glow and Mauna Kea stargazing.

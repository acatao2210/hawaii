Hapuna Beach + Mauna Kea Stargazing

The best beach on the Big Island in the morning. The top of the world — and the universe — at night.

🏖️
Hapuna Beach
Beach

Consistently rated one of the best beaches in the United States — a half-mile crescent of soft white sand with brilliant turquoise water and reliably good swimming conditions. There's a lifeguard on duty, clean facilities, and enough space that it never feels crowded. The water is clear enough to see tropical fish without snorkel gear. Arrive by 9 AM to get a prime spot.

Parking $10 entry fee per vehicle. Parking does fill up midday on weekends — arrive early or use the overflow lot on the road. The adjacent Waialea Beach (Beach 69) is a beautiful, quieter alternative a mile south.

Afternoon Rest + Warm Layers
Culture

Rest up in the afternoon — you'll be up late for stargazing. Use this time for a late lunch, any last souvenir shopping in Kailua-Kona, or just a nap. Before heading to Mauna Kea, have a solid meal (the summit is cold and energy-intensive), and dress in your warmest layers. Seriously — it can be below 40°F at 13,796 feet.

What to bring Pack: fleece or heavy jacket, wool socks, beanie, gloves, an extra layer. Bring snacks and a thermos of hot tea/coffee. Your rental car needs a full tank of gas. The road is paved to the summit but steep — let your engine cool on the way down.

Mauna Kea Summit + Stargazing
Must-do

The highest point in the Pacific and the site of the world's largest telescope complex. The view above the clouds is one of the most alien and beautiful things you'll ever see. The Visitor Information Station at 9,200 ft offers free stargazing programs with telescopes (usually 6–10 PM). Drive to the summit for sunset — the shadow of Mauna Kea stretches to the horizon. When the stars come out, the Milky Way is so bright it looks painted.

Acclimatize Spend at least 30 minutes at the Visitor Information Station (9,200 ft) before driving to the summit — altitude sickness is real. Drink water, no alcohol. The summit road requires 4WD in some conditions but is usually accessible in a standard car. Check road conditions at mkwc.ifa.hawaii.edu.

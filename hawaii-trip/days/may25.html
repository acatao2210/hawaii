Hawaiʻi Volcanoes National Park

The most otherworldly landscape on earth — active lava, ancient craters, and jungle to ocean in one day.

🌋
Kilauea Visitor Center — Park Entry
Culture

Arrive early — the park is 45 minutes from Hilo. Stop at the Visitor Center to pick up a map, check the latest eruption status (Kilauea has been active intermittently), and get oriented. Rangers will tell you exactly where active lava activity is visible today, if any.

Entry fee The $30 vehicle pass is valid for 7 days — perfect since you're on the island until the 28th. nps.gov/havo has current eruption activity status updated daily.

Kilauea Iki Crater Trail
Hiking Must-do

The crown jewel of hiking in the park. A 4-mile loop that descends 400 feet through a rainforest, then crosses the floor of Kilauea Iki — a crater that had a lava lake in 1959. You're literally walking across solidified lava while steam vents hiss around you. The surface is uneven and eerie, like walking on the moon. Surreal.

Trail conditions The trail can be slippery in wet conditions — wear sturdy shoes. The crater floor is exposed with no shade. Cairns mark the trail across the lava. Takes about 2 hours at a moderate pace. Go counterclockwise for the best flow.

Thurston Lava Tube (Nahuku)
Must-do

A 500-year-old lava tube — a tunnel formed when the outer crust of a lava flow hardened while molten rock continued to flow through the inside, eventually draining and leaving a hollow tube. The main lit section is a short, dramatic walk through a cathedral of ancient rock surrounded by a lush ohia lehua forest.

Extension The main tube is short and lit, but there's an unlit extension beyond it — bring a flashlight for a more adventurous experience. Not for the claustrophobic.

Chain of Craters Road
Must-do

A 19-mile drive from the summit of Kilauea down to the ocean — dropping 3,700 feet through ancient lava fields to dramatic sea cliffs. The road ends where a 1990 lava flow buried it completely. Along the way are pullouts for pit craters, ancient Hawaiian petroglyphs, and a natural stone arch where waves crash into a lava bench. Otherworldly.

Petroglyphs Stop at the Pu'u Loa Petroglyphs pullout (about 1.5 miles each way on a boardwalk trail) — over 23,000 ancient Hawaiian rock carvings, the largest collection in Hawaii.

Lava Glow at Dusk
Must-do

If Kilauea is actively erupting (check nps.gov/havo that morning), you can see the glow of the lava lake inside Halema'uma'u Crater from the Kilauea Overlook at night. Even when not erupting, the sulfuric glow, steam vents, and star-filled sky above the caldera are extraordinary. Dress warmly — it's 4,000 feet elevation and gets cold after dark.

Eruption status Check the USGS Hawaiian Volcano Observatory (volcanoes.usgs.gov) the morning of for real-time eruption activity. When active, the lava glow can be seen from multiple overlooks around the caldera.
